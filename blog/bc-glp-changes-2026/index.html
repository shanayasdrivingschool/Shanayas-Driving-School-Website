BC Is Removing the Second Road Test: What Changes on October 19, 2026

ICBC is replacing BC's second road test with a Driving Record Assessment on October 19, 2026. What changes, who it affects, and what to do now.

By Shanaya's Driving School · Updated July 21, 2026 · 9 min read · ICBC Updates

On July 20, 2026, the Government of British Columbia and ICBC announced the largest change to the Graduated Licensing Program (GLP) in more than 25 years. The headline: the second road test is being removed. From October 19, 2026, drivers will move from a novice licence to a Class 5 through a records check rather than a second drive with an examiner.

Below is a plain-language breakdown of what is changing, who it affects, and the steps worth taking before October. Everything here is drawn from the official Government of B.C. news release and ICBC's own GLP changes page, both linked at the end.

The short version

  • The second road test (novice to Class 5) is replaced by an in-office Driving Record Assessment, which checks your record rather than your driving.
  • Passing it no longer gives you an unrestricted licence. You get a Class 5 with a 12-month zero alcohol and drug restriction first.
  • Drivers 25 and older get a shorter path: nine months in the learner stage instead of 12, and 12 months as a novice instead of 24.
  • For drivers under 25, the timeline does not change. It is still 12 months as a learner and 24 months as a novice, reduced to 18 with an approved GLP course.
  • The first road test, the one that takes you from L to N, is not going anywhere. It is now the only road test in the program.

What the Driving Record Assessment actually is

It is not a driving test. It is an in-office check of your driving record to confirm you have spent the required time driving safely. To upgrade from a Class 7 or 7,8 licence to a Class 5 or 5,6, ICBC states you must meet all of the following:

  • At least 24 months of safe driving if you are under 25, or 18 months if you completed an approved GLP course during your learner stage.
  • At least 12 months of safe driving if you are 25 or older.
  • No convictions for excessive speed or use of electronic devices during that period.
  • No prohibitions or suspensions during that period.

The trade-off is worth understanding. You no longer have to perform on one nerve-wracking morning, but your record now has to stay clean for a two-year stretch. A single excessive speeding ticket or distracted driving conviction can restart the novice period. Under the old system, a bad ticket was a fine. Under the new one, it can cost you years.

The new stages, side by side

The old model had three stages. The new one has four, because the Class 5 you receive after the assessment comes with strings attached for the first year.

The old model

  1. Learner (Class 7L), 12 months
  2. First road test, then Novice (Class 7), 24 months, or 18 with an approved course
  3. Second road test, then full Class 5 with no restrictions

The new model, drivers under 25

  1. Learner, 12 months
  2. First road test, then Novice, 24 months, or 18 with an approved GLP course
  3. Driving Record Assessment, then Class 5 with a zero alcohol and drug restriction for 12 months
  4. Class 5 without the restriction

The new model, drivers 25 and older

  1. Learner, 9 months
  2. First road test, then Novice, 12 months
  3. Driving Record Assessment, then Class 5 with a zero alcohol and drug restriction for 12 months
  4. Class 5 without the restriction

If you have a road test booked for on or after October 19

It will be cancelled. ICBC has confirmed that second road tests scheduled on or after October 19, 2026 will be cancelled automatically, and that affected drivers will receive an email confirming the cancellation and explaining the next steps.

You are not losing anything by having it cancelled, since the assessment replaces it. But if you would rather finish under the current process, you can still take a Class 5 road test before October 19. Appointments in the Victoria area book up well in advance, so that window is narrower than it looks.

If you are close to eligible and want your Class 5 the old way, book now. If you are not close, there is nothing to rush, because the assessment will be waiting for you.

The changes that make learning more accessible

Alongside the road test change, several rules are loosening:

  • Supervisor age drops from 25 to 22. More people in your life can legally supervise your practice driving, which is a real difference for families where no one was old enough before.
  • The age of consent to apply for a licence drops from 19 to 18, so more young adults can apply independently.
  • Passenger restrictions gain an immediate family exemption, which helps novice drivers in larger families.
  • The Province says the Driving Record Assessment will be available at Service BC offices, driver licensing agent offices and ICBC driver licensing offices, reducing travel for people in rural and remote communities. ICBC's own page is narrower on this point, so see the note at the end of this article before you travel to a non-ICBC location.

The Province has been explicit that a major goal here is access for First Nations, rural and remote communities, where getting to a road test appointment has been a genuine barrier.

What ICBC told driving schools

As an ICBC-recognised driving school, we also received the July 2026 driver training industry bulletin, which included a few operational details worth passing along:

  • The road test fee rises from $35 to $50. ICBC describes this as a $35 saving because you no longer pay for a second test. Netted against the higher fee, the real difference is about $20: two tests at $35 used to cost $70, and one test at $50 now costs $50.
  • The Class 5/7 marking sheet moves to a demerit-based approach with error cut-offs that flag poor driving behaviours.
  • Road test appointments get five extra minutes so examiners can give more feedback. The drive itself is not getting longer.
  • Starting in early fall 2026, drivers currently in the GLP will receive letters from ICBC explaining how they are affected.

The Province has also indicated that some eligible drivers may qualify for a one-time automatic upgrade from Class 7 to Class 5 without needing to complete an in-office assessment. Details on exactly who qualifies had not been published at the time of writing, so watch for your ICBC letter.

Why the approved GLP course matters more now, not less

It would be easy to read "the second road test is gone" as "driver training matters less." The opposite is true, for three reasons.

First, the course discount is now the only shortcut left in the program. For drivers under 25, completing an ICBC-approved GLP course during the learner stage cuts the novice period from 24 months to 18. That is six months earlier to a Class 5, and it is the single remaining way to move faster.

Two conditions are worth reading carefully. ICBC specifies the course must be completed during the learner phase, so leaving it until after your first road test may mean it does not count. And the industry bulletin is blunt that if you start but do not finish an approved course, you still owe the full 24 months. Enrolling is not enough. Finishing is.

Second, your record now carries the weight the exit test used to. There is no longer a final exam where good driving can prove itself. Instead, two years of clean driving is the proof. Habits built early are what protect that record.

Third, the one remaining road test is now the whole gate. Failing the Class 7 test costs you time and a rebooking fee, and it is the only driving assessment standing between you and a licence. Preparing properly for it matters more than it did when there was a second chance later.

What to do now

  1. If you are a learner under 25: get into an approved GLP course during your learner stage and finish it. Six months is a meaningful head start.
  2. If you are a learner 25 or older: you reach a Class 5 about 15 months sooner, in 21 months instead of 36. Note that it is a restricted Class 5, so the fully unrestricted licence arrives at 33 months rather than 36. Focus on the first road test, since it is the only one you will take.
  3. If you are a novice with a Class 5 test booked before October 19: decide whether to keep it or let the assessment handle it. Either route works.
  4. If you are a novice with a test booked on or after October 19: expect the cancellation email and confirm your assessment eligibility date.
  5. Everyone: protect your record. Excessive speeding and phone-use convictions now carry consequences they did not before.

What this means in Greater Victoria

The rules are province-wide, but a few effects land differently depending on where you learn to drive.

The Victoria and Saanich test routes matter more than they used to. With the second road test gone, the Class 7 test on those routes becomes the only time an examiner ever assesses your driving. The hills, one-way streets, roundabouts, and heavy cyclist traffic that make the local routes demanding are now the whole gate rather than the first of two. If you are learning in Victoria, Saanich, Langford, or Colwood, that is an argument for preparing on the actual routes rather than generally.

The booking squeeze is local. If you want to finish under the old process, you need a Class 5 test before October 19, and Greater Victoria appointments are booked well ahead at the best of times. Expect that to get worse as the date approaches and eligible novice drivers try to get in ahead of the change.

Island and Westshore drivers gain the most. The Province was explicit that a major goal is reducing travel for people in rural, remote, and Indigenous communities. For students on Salt Spring Island, or out in Sooke and Metchosin, the difference between booking a road test appointment and completing an in-office records check is real time and ferry cost saved.
